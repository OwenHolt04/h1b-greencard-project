# Remotion Visual Suggestions v2

For the MGT 120 H-1B → Green Card future-state demo

## Purpose

This document is meant to guide the Claude session working on the Remotion video. It is **directional guidance**, not rigid law. The creative direction may still evolve. The goal is to provide a strong set of visual ideas, motion patterns, storytelling tricks, and implementation guidance that help the video feel:

- more narrative
- more capability-focused
- more emotionally legible
- more engaging in class
- less like a website walkthrough
- less like a technical product showcase

This video should support the locked CaseBridge narrative and the finalized app states. It should stay faithful to the workbook-backed pain point → capability logic while still allowing room for strong cinematic choices.

---

## Working intent

The video should ideally feel less like:

- a screen recording of a website
- a tour of full static screens
- a technical UI showcase
- a generic startup product ad

And more like:

- a guided story through Prajwal’s case
- a visual proof of capabilities
- a sequence of pain point → capability → result moments
- a polished presentation artifact built to hold attention in class

The site is still important, but in the video it should behave more like a **cinematic story environment** than a normal app UI.

---

## North star

Follow **Prajwal Kulkarni’s** case from fragmented current-state chaos into one shared **CaseBridge** record, then prove value through specific workbook-backed capabilities:

- unified case visibility
- shared field sync across forms
- pre-filing validation
- role-based coordination
- deadline protection

The video is a **case story**, not a product tour. Every beat should earn its existence by resolving a specific pain the audience just felt.

---

The extra time should be used for:

## Core video philosophy suggestions

### 1. Progressive disclosure is better than full-screen explanation

Do not present an entire interface and hope the audience notices the right thing.

Instead:

- reveal one meaningful region
- focus attention there
- animate the capability
- show the resulting change
- move to the next meaningful region

### 2. Let the UI be the stage, not the whole story

The UI should support the story, but the story should lead. The audience should feel like they are following:

- Prajwal’s confusion and risk
- the capability that helps
- the visible change
- the system-wide benefit

### 3. Each capability needs a proof moment

A capability lands best when it has a visible cause-and-effect beat, such as:

- one field updates, multiple forms sync
- one issue is caught, readiness improves
- one role switch changes priorities
- one deadline alert creates a protective action

### 4. Motion should guide meaning

Animations are most useful when they direct attention or prove something. They are less useful when they are just decorative.

### 5. One dominant focal area per scene

At any moment, only one region should really matter.

If four widgets are competing equally for attention, the scene is too noisy.

## Beat-by-beat visual suggestions

## Beat 1: Meet Prajwal / Current-State Chaos

**Goal:** create emotional tension quickly.

### What the audience should feel
“This looks fragmented, risky, and exhausting.”

### What should be shown
- Prajwal identity line:
  - Prajwal Kulkarni
  - Senior PM
  - HPE
  - H-1B
  - EB-2 India
- floating fragments of:
  - ETA-9089
  - I-140
  - I-485
  - I-765
  - I-131
  - G-28
  - DOL portal
  - USCIS portal
  - DOS portal
  - email chain
  - mail / notice snippet
- red circles / red callouts appearing over pain points
- short pain text fragments such as:
  - duplicate entry
  - unclear status
  - waiting on attorney
  - deadline risk
  - same case, different systems
  - what happens next?

### Suggested visual treatment
- layered stacks of forms, notices, emails, portal labels
- overlapping cards with slight rotation and motion drift
- fragmented arrows that do not converge
- blur/sharp contrast where only certain terms are readable
- quick focus shifts between disconnected systems
- subtle instability or drift in the camera

### Strong end frame for this beat
- “3 agencies. 6 forms. 0 dashboards.”
- subline: “One inconsistency can reset months of progress.”

### Avoid
- product UI
- app chrome
- full dashboard
- dense policy explanation
- long text blocks

## Beat 2: CaseBridge Appears

**Goal:** make the transition from chaos to order feel earned.

### What the audience should feel
“Finally, there is structure.”

### What should be shown
- dark pause or reset
- scattered current-state artifacts collapse / dissolve / align
- CaseBridge title / lockup
- one shared case record frame
- case header appears with:
  - Prajwal
  - HPE
  - current preparation stage
  - 102 days remaining

### Suggested visual treatment
- fragmented elements collapsing into one centered frame
- interface resolving from blur to sharp focus
- subtle grid/alignment animation
- calm push-in toward the case header

### Important note
Do **not** show a generic homepage hero with marketing fluff or CTA buttons.

The reveal should feel like:
- one record
- one workspace
- one source of truth

## Beat 3: One Workspace, Full Journey

**Goal:** make “single source of truth” feel real.

### What the audience should understand
The case is now visible across the full journey, instead of across disconnected systems.

### What should be shown
- case header
- journey timeline
- DOL / USCIS / DOS status blocks
- readiness score
- plain-language next step
- alerts:
  - H-1B expires in 102 days
  - priority date current
  - attorney review pending

### Suggested visual treatment
Reveal in layers:
1. case identity
2. timeline left-to-right
3. DOL / USCIS / DOS statuses
4. next-step card
5. alerts

### Useful motion ideas
- spotlight mask around the case header
- timeline reveal left-to-right
- agency status blocks animating in sequentially
- soft dimming of secondary UI
- connector lines landing on the one shared record

### Avoid
- showing the whole dashboard equally
- dense case tables
- multiple case lists
- intake detail
- validation results

---

## Beat 4: Enter Once, Sync Everywhere

**Goal:** prove the shared intake capability clearly.

### What the audience should understand
Shared case data is entered once and reused across multiple forms, reducing re-keying and contradiction risk.

### What should be shown
- one source field, ideally:
  - Employer Legal Name
- left side:
  - shared intake sections
- right side:
  - filing package / form cards
- “synced to N forms” style indicators
- visible sync relationships

### Suggested visual treatment
- isolate one intake field
- animate the typed or edited value
- send a pulse / wave / connector line into downstream form cards
- update 2 to 4 destination surfaces in sequence
- show subtle synced confirmations

### Best visual metaphor
**source node → downstream nodes**

### Avoid
- full PDF form reading
- dense supporting-doc detail
- validation issues yet
- too many visible intake sections at once

---

## Beat 5: Catch Errors Before Filing

**Goal:** deliver the strongest proof moment in the whole video.

### What the audience should understand
The system catches a contradiction before filing, not months later during adjudication. One fix updates multiple forms and improves readiness.

### Required visible sequence
1. validation starts
2. scan / analyze motion
3. issues appear
4. employer-name inconsistency becomes the dominant issue
5. affected fields and forms highlight
6. fix is made once
7. issue resolves
8. readiness score rises

### Specific content to preserve
- only **3 issues total**
- employer-name inconsistency is the visually dominant one
- remaining issues are secondary
- readiness visibly improves:
  - **72 → 80**
- 3 forms visibly update

### Suggested visual treatment
- clean analyzing state
- scan line across form package
- issue cards drop in with severity emphasis
- line or spatial link between issue card and affected fields
- blur everything except the problem field and issue panel
- score number morph upward after fix
- blocker / issue card morphs into resolved state

### Avoid
- more than 3 issues
- long legal explanation
- multiple equally important fixes
- giving equal weight to all issues

This scene should be engineered as the strongest, clearest “proof of value” moment in the entire video.

## Beat 6: Same Case, Three Stakeholders

**Goal:** prove stakeholder alignment without breaking the single-source-of-truth idea.

### What the audience should understand
The same case record supports different stakeholder priorities without creating different truths.

### Order
1. Applicant
2. Employer / HR
3. Attorney

### What each role should emphasize

#### Applicant
- plain-language next step
- reassurance
- completion visibility
- current status
- reduced jargon

#### Employer / HR
- deadline risk
- sponsorship / extension cost visibility
- operational continuity
- responsible actions

#### Attorney
- blocker severity
- evidence gaps
- filing readiness
- legal review needs

### Suggested visual treatment
- keep the same case header fixed
- use the role switcher as an anchor
- reorder surrounding cards rather than just swapping text
- slightly shift visual emphasis / tone by role
- animate role priorities in, not as a hard cut

### Avoid
- three giant different products
- identical layouts with only text changes
- legal-heavy applicant view
- too many subpanels at once

The core visual message should be:
**same case, different needs**

## Beat 7: Deadline Protection

**Goal:** make operational value visible.

### What the audience should understand
The system does not just display information. It helps surface and manage deadline risk before it becomes a crisis.

### What should be shown
- 102-day countdown
- visible auto-trigger threshold at 90 days
- employer task
- attorney task
- cost difference between rush vs proactive handling
- risk shift:
  - at risk → managed / on track

### Suggested visual treatment
- zoom toward the deadline / alert region
- warning card grows into focus
- reminder / task cards animate into queues
- risk state visibly improves
- cost comparison is brief and readable

### Avoid
- full filing workflow
- legal rule explanation
- too much operational detail
- making it feel like only a notification demo

This should feel like **business/process value**, not just “the UI sent an alert.”

---

## Beat 8: What the Portal Changes vs What Still Requires Reform

**Goal:** increase credibility and avoid overclaiming.

### What the audience should understand
The portal directly improves process friction, but it does not solve statutory scarcity.

### What should be shown
Two clear columns:

#### Left: solves directly
Examples:
- reduces rework
- clarifies status
- aligns stakeholders
- catches avoidable errors earlier
- reduces deadline surprises
- reduces redundant re-entry

#### Right: still requires reform
Examples:
- visa caps
- retrogression
- country limits
- staffing constraints
- broader policy / legislative issues

### Suggested visual treatment
- clean split layout
- left side brightens first
- right side enters more quietly
- restrained iconography
- elegant, not defensive

### Tone note
This scene should feel:
- thoughtful
- credible
- strong

Not apologetic.

A useful phrase:
**Process help, not magic.**

---

## Beat 9: Impact Close

**Goal:** leave one clear, memorable takeaway.

### What the audience should understand
Better coordination is where better process starts.

### What should be shown
- one composed final frame
- calm visual state
- final impact line
- 4 key metrics only

### Suggested metrics
- RFE rate: ~25% → <5%
- Status portals: 4 → 1
- Attorney hours: ~8 → ~3
- Extension cost: $5K+ → ~$2.5K

### Possible closing lines
- One source of truth for a fragmented journey
- Reduce rework. Clarify status. Align stakeholders.
- A better process starts with better coordination.

### Suggested visual treatment
- gentle pullback
- subtle settle
- stable final title / lockup
- no new information introduced here

## Suggested animation primitives to reuse

These are not requirements. They are reusable building blocks that are likely useful across scenes.

### Focus mask
Dim the rest of the screen and highlight one region.

Useful for:
- issue detection
- next-step card
- deadline chip
- single source field

### Guided zoom
Zoom into one meaningful area instead of cutting immediately.

Useful for:
- moving into the shared record
- moving into the workspace
- moving from form stack to issue field

### Spatial continuity pan
Pan between related regions so the audience understands the connection.

Useful for:
- source field → downstream forms
- alert → task queue
- issue card → affected field → readiness score

### Sequential reveal
Bring related elements in one at a time.

Useful for:
- timeline
- status blocks
- issue steps
- role priorities

### Sync pulse
A value change sends visible feedback into dependent surfaces.

Useful for:
- field sync
- multi-form updates
- cross-surface propagation

### Score morph
Animate metric changes instead of hard-swapping numbers.

Useful for:
- readiness score
- issue count
- risk score

### Connector lines / path animation
Use thin, elegant lines to explain relationships.

Useful for:
- one record powering many forms
- cross-agency visibility
- stakeholder alignment
- issue-source linkage

### Card morph / status transition
Transform one state into another instead of fully cutting.

Useful for:
- blocker → resolved
- at risk → managed
- pending review → ready

### Kinetic captions
Very short on-screen phrases timed to proof moments.

Examples:
- Entered once
- Caught earlier
- Same case, different needs
- Deadline made visible
- Process help, not magic

Use sparingly.

## Camera suggestions

Helpful approaches:
- slow push-ins
- shallow pans
- cropped focus windows
- soft drift in tension scenes
- steady lock-on during proof moments
- controlled pullback at the end

Less helpful approaches:
- random zooming
- excessive whip pans
- long static full-screen shots
- cursor wandering around the UI

### Cursor guidance
Cursor can still be useful, but ideally only when it:
- triggers validation
- edits the employer-name field
- switches roles
- opens an important state

The cursor should not be the main storyteller.

## What not to overdo

A few anti-patterns to avoid:

### 1. Full-screen static dashboard shots
These feel like website tours, not narrative demonstrations.

### 2. Reading-heavy scenes
If the viewer has to read paragraphs, the scene is underperforming.

### 3. Motion with no communicative purpose
Animation should guide, connect, or prove something.

### 4. Equal emphasis everywhere
Each scene should have one dominant focal area.

### 5. Too many flashy transitions
The tone should still feel credible, calm, and institutional.

### 6. Decorative complexity from old app states
Do not accidentally reintroduce:
- document checklist
- evidence vault
- AI triage section
- stage-advance toy interactions

Those are backup/Q&A territory, not main-path video content.

## Workbook fidelity reminder

For each major scene, the Remotion session should be able to answer:

1. **What pain is being shown?**
2. **What capability is being demonstrated?**
3. **What visibly changes because of it?**
4. **Who benefits?**
5. **Which workbook capability row(s) does this correspond to?**

If a scene cannot answer those clearly, it probably needs a stronger proof moment.

The main-path video should prioritize the workbook capabilities that are:
- high-value
- visually demonstrable
- central to the locked narrative

Examples:
- unified dashboard
- auto-populated shared fields
- pre-flight validation
- handoff-stage checks
- role-based plain-language guidance
- milestone visibility
- deadline protection

Use the video for:
- the 9 locked beats

## Scene acceptance checklist

Each scene should pass these questions.

### Narrative
- What pain is being shown?
- What capability is being introduced?
- What changed on screen because of it?
- Who benefits?

### Visual
- Is there one dominant focal area?
- Is the motion guiding attention?
- Would the scene still read without voiceover?

### Demo / implementation
- Is the state deterministic?
- Is it easy to capture cleanly?
- Does the scene enter and exit smoothly?
