Currently, this process is much more complex than one case should be.

It feels like fragments.

Forms.
Portals.
Emails.
Physical mail.
Deadlines.
And no single place to see the full picture.

For Prajwal, that means a path that becomes slow, unclear, and frustrating for him, his employer, and his attorney.

He is a software engineer from India, working in the U.S. on an H-1B visa.
He has an employer willing to sponsor him.
He has an attorney.
And still, his case is spread across three federal agencies, four different portals, six overlapping forms, and more than 165 pages of instructions.

The same name, date of birth, A-number, and employer details get entered again and again.
And even after checking DOL, USCIS, the visa bulletin, and his mailbox, he still doesn't know whether his case has a problem or is just waiting in line.

That is the current state.
Fragmented.
Repetitive.
And hard to navigate.

But Prajwal, his employer, and his attorney all deserve a process that works better than this.
So here is what that looks like.

Instead of treating this as a stack of disconnected filings, everything centers on one shared case record.

One workspace for Prajwal's case.
One place to see timeline, agency progress, alerts, and next steps.
Not four portals and a mailbox.
One source of truth.

And because the record is shared, information only has to be entered once.

His employer's legal name.
His A-number.
The FEIN.
The SOC code.
Job details.
Wage data.

Fields that today get repeated across the ETA-9089, I-140, I-485, and related filings — often months apart — now populate across the filing package from a single entry.

That matters because repeated entry is exactly how contradictions build up.

And the biggest shift happens before anything gets filed.

The system checks for inconsistencies before submission.

A title on the I-140 that doesn't match what was filed on the ETA-9089.
A wage classification that conflicts with the prevailing wage determination.
An employer name written two different ways across forms.

In today's process, issues like that don't surface until months later, when USCIS sends back a Request for Evidence, adding two to four months and thousands of dollars in additional legal fees.

Here, they are caught before filing.

And when the attorney corrects the problem once in the shared record, that correction carries through to every form it touches.

That is how you move from reactive cleanup to proactive quality control.
And that is how mismatch-driven RFEs drop from about twenty-five percent to under five.

Now, the same case still serves different people.

Prajwal sees plain-language guidance:
where he is,
what is next,
and what he actually needs to do.

His employer sees deadlines, extension costs, and continuity risk — because every month of delay can trigger another H-1B extension at four to ten thousand dollars.

His attorney sees filing readiness, evidence gaps, and legal blockers — and spends less time answering status questions the dashboard already handles.

Same case.
Same truth.
Different priorities, surfaced clearly for each stakeholder.

And when Prajwal's H-1B is approaching expiration, that doesn't sit in someone's inbox until it becomes a crisis.

It is surfaced early.
Ninety days out.
With enough lead time for the employer and attorney to act.

So what does that change?

Validation catches mismatches before filing, not at adjudication.
Status becomes visible across the full journey, not scattered across four portals.
Attorney time shifts from routine calls to legal judgment.
Extension risk is managed early, not discovered late.

And that does not solve everything.

It does not change visa caps.
It does not fix retrogression.
It does not erase the structural backlog.

Those remain systemic issues.

But even within that reality, avoidable friction still matters.

A filing error that adds four months and five thousand dollars in legal fees — that is avoidable.
A missed extension deadline that puts someone's work authorization at risk — that is avoidable.
And a process where three stakeholders can finally see the same case, in the same place, at the same time — that is a meaningful improvement in a system that right now offers very little of it.
